The Schrödinger Equation

The Schrödinger equation is a statement on realisable quantum states in the real world. So a solution to the Schrödinger equation is a function that describes the state of some quantum object.
In order to make an analogy to classical mechanics lets consider kinetic energy, i.e. $$E_{kin}=\frac{1}{2}mv^2=\frac{p^2}{2m}$$ Is quantum mechanics the momentum operator is represented by the gradient \(\hat{p}=-i\hbar\nabla\) which is just the partial derivative in one dimension, i.e. \(\hat{p}=-i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\). Now we are ready to write the Schrödinger equation which is a statement on the time development as it relates to the mechanical energy of the physical system, \begin{align} i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\Psi(x,t)=&E_{mec}\Psi(x,t)\\ =&(E_{kin}+E_{pot})\Psi(x,t)\\ =&\left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}+V(x,t)\right] \Psi(x,t) \end{align} The thing then, that dictates our quantum state is the potential and I will now investigate some simple potentials under the condition that the potential not depend on time and that the states are seperable in time and space.

Seperation of Variables

A state that is seperable can be written as $$\Psi(x,t)=\psi(x)\varphi(t)$$ This has the consequence that I can investigate the time and space components of the Schrödinger equation seperately because \begin{align} &&i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\Psi(x,t)=&\left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}+V(x,t)\right]\Psi(x,t)\\ \implies&&i\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t}=& \frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial x^2}+V\Psi\\ \implies&&\psi i\hbar\frac{d\varphi}{dt}=&\varphi\left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}+V\psi\right)\\ \implies&&\frac{1}{\varphi}i\hbar\frac{d\varphi}{dt}=&\frac{1}{\psi}\left( -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}+V\psi\right)\\ &&=&E \end{align} Since the variables are implied we disregard them as convenient and at the very end we have an equation where each side only depends on a different variable so they must both be equal to the same constant, which I will call \(E\) for inexplicable reasons. This has two immediate consequences, $$\frac{\varphi'}{\varphi}=\frac{E}{i\hbar}\implies\varphi(t)∝ e^{Et/i\hbar}=e^{-iEt/\hbar}$$ We will absorb the proportionality constant into the full state so it will be omitted in the following sections. The second consequence is that $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}+V\psi=E\psi$$ which is called the the time independent Schrödinger equation and it's important to note that \(V\) is not simply some constant like \(E\), but it's a function of \(x\).

Statistical Interpretation

Born's statistical interpretation of the wave function of a quantum state states that the integral of the squared magnitude is the probability distribution function for the location of the quantum object. This means that $$\int_R|\Psi(\underline{x},t)|^2d\underline{x}=P(X\in R)$$ where \(X\in R\) means that the quantum object is in that specific region. This then implies that $$\int_{\mathbb{R}^3}|\Psi(\underline{x},t)|^2d\underline{x}=1$$ which is often used to "normalize" wave functions, which often involves determining the coefficient of the wave function, including the one from the seperation of variables.

Infinite Square Well

The infinite square well is defined as the potential \(V(x,t)=0\) for \(x \in[0,a]\) and \(V(x,t)=\infty\) for \(a\notin[0,a]\). This essentially means that the quantum object cannot exsist outside the region bounded by the interval. The time independent Schrödinger equation then becomes \begin{align} &&-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}=&E\psi\\ \implies&&\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}=&-\frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}\psi=-k^2\psi\\ \implies&&\psi(x)=&A\sin(kx)+B\cos(kx) \end{align} By imposing continuity restrictions on our states we know that \(\psi(0)= \psi(a)=0\) so $$0=A\sin(0)+B\cos(0)\implies B=0$$ and \begin{align} &&0=&A\sin(ka)\\ \implies&&k=&\frac{2\pi n}{a}\\ \implies&&\frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}=&\frac{4\pi^2n^2}{a^2}\\ \implies&&E=&\frac{2}{m}\left(\frac{\pi n\hbar}{a}\right)^2 \end{align} for \(n\in\mathbb{N}\). Now I'll normalize the state to determine \(A\) \begin{align} 1=&\int_\mathbb{R}|\Psi(x,t)|^2dx\\ =&\int_0^a|Ae^{-iEt/\hbar}\sin(kx)|^2dx\\ =&|A|^2\int_0^a\sin^2(kx)dx \end{align} To integrate \(\sin^2\) we use the fact that \(\sin^2+\cos^2=1\) and that the area of \(\cos\) and \(\sin\) have the same magnitude on intervals that are whole multiples of the half-period, which is exactly the case here because the \(k\) was chosen to make \(x=a\) be a root of the sine which happens at multiples of half-periods. This yield the following calculation \begin{align} &&\int_0^a\sin^2=&\int_0^a\sin^2+\cos^2-\int_0^a\cos^2\\ \implies&&\int_0^a\sin^2+\int_0^a\cos^2=&\int_0^a\\ \implies&&2\int_0^a\sin^2=&a\\ \implies&&\int_0^a\sin^2=&\frac{a}{2} \end{align} which in turn means that \begin{align} &&|A|=&\sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\\ \implies&&A=&\sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}e^{i\theta}\\ \implies&&\Psi(x,t)=&\sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}e^{i\left(\theta-\frac{2\hbar}{m} \left(\frac{\pi n}{a}\right)^2t\right)} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi n}{a}x\right) \end{align}
