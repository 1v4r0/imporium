Ordinary Differential Equations

Differential equations are equations that describe the development of some system and often includes the derivatives of the parameters of the system.

Exponential Growth

The first equation we will work with is exponential growth, where the slope at any point is proportional to the height, or the y-coordinate, i.e. $$y'=ay$$ This equation is independent of \(x\) and horizontally constant and has an asymptote at \(y=0\). The general solution to this equation is easily shown to be $$y=ce^{ax}$$ where \(c\in\mathbb{R}\) is some constant that is determined by some initial or boundary condition. I will prove uniqueness below.

Translated Exponential Growth

The second equation is related to the first, where the slope is a linear function in the height, or y-coordinate, $$y'=b-ay$$ which is also independent of the x-coordinate and has an asymptote at \(y=b/a\), with a solution which is that same translation of exponential growth, i.e. $$y=\frac{b}{a}-ce^{ax}$$ where \(c\) again determines the initial/boundary conditions.

Logistic Growth

In the third and final equation for now the slope is a quadractic in the height(y-coordinate), and a product of the two previous slopes, i.e. $$y'=y(b-ay)=ay(M-y)$$ It has inherited the two previous asymptotes, one at \(y=0\) and one at \(y=M=b/a\). It has slightly exponential characteristics, but these are mostly visual. The solution to this equation is either \(y=0\) or $$y=\frac{M}{1-ce^{aMx}}$$ which includes the solution \(y=M\) if \(c=0\).

Proof of Uniqueness

Lets start with exponential growth and assume we have a solution \(y\). Then we multiply this solution by \(e^{-ax}\) and differentiate. $$(ye^{-ax})'=y'e^{-ax}-aye^{-ax}=e^{-ax}(ay-ay)=0$$ The fact that the derivative is zero, means whatever we differentiated is a constant, i.e. $$c=ye^{-ax}\iff y=ce^{ax}$$ Now for the translated version, we assume a solution \(y\) and differentiate the following $$(b-ay)'=-ay'=-a(b-ay)$$ which means that when I differentiate it I get the same thing back multiplied by some constant, i.e. it's exponential growth, so $$b-ay=ce^{-ax}\iff y=\frac{b}{a}-\frac{c}{a}e^{-ax}$$ but the constant divided by \(a\) is just another constant. For logistic growth consider one of its solutions \(y\) and the following derivative $$\left(\frac{1}{y}\right)'=-\frac{y'}{y^2}=-\frac{ay(M-y)}{y^2}=a-aM\frac{1}{y}$$ So this function is a solution to translated exponential growth, i.e. $$\frac{1}{y}=\frac{a}{aM}-ce^{-aM}\iff y=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{M}-ce^{-aMx}}= \frac{M}{1-Mce^{-aMx}}$$ where again a constant multiplied by another, is just a new constant.

∎

Proof of Existance

Now i will prove that the expression is a solution, first i differentiate $$y'=-\frac{M}{(1-ce^{-aMx})^2}(-ce^{-aMx})(-aM)=-\frac{aM^2ce^{-aMx}}{(1-ce^{-aMx})^2}$$ Then I calculate the right side $$ay(M-y)=\frac{aM}{1-ce^{-aMx}}\left(M-\frac{M}{1-ce^{-aMx}}\right)= \frac{aM^2}{1-ce^{-aMx}}\frac{1-ce^{-aMx}-1}{1-ce^{-aMx}}= -\frac{aM^2ce^{-aMx}}{(1-ce^{-aMx})^2}$$

∎
