This section is aimed at students in upper secondary education in the Danish school system, some objects will be simplified and some details will be omitted.

Prime Numbers

The prime numbers are the non-units of the natural numbers that are not divisible by other non-units.
In this case, the numbers 0 and 1 are the additive and multiplicative "units".

Prime Factorization

Each non-unit can be uniquely be represented by the product of primes.
This in effect means that for any non-unit \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) can be written as $$n=\prod_{p\text{ prime}}p^{k_p}$$ for example $$120=2^3⋅3⋅5$$ so \(k_2=3\) and \(k_3=k_5=1\) and \(k_p=0\) for all other primes.

Euler's Totient Function

Euler's totient function of a number \(\phi(n)\), counts the numbers \(m< n\) that are co-prime with the original number.
The totient function of a prime is always one lower than its value, since the only numbers it's not co-prime with are numbers which have it in its prime factorization, which will be larger than the prime, i.e. \(\phi(p) =p-1\). For the product of two primes, then the totient function of the product is the product of the totient functions, i.e. \(\phi(pq)=(p-1)(q-1)\).

Remainders

For two numbers \(a\) and \(n\), they can be written in terms of each other as $$n=qa+r$$ where \(q\in\mathbb{N}\) is called the quotient and \(r< a\) is called the remainder. The mathematical notation for this is $$a\equiv r\mod n$$

Euler's Theorem

For two co-prime integers \(a\) and \(n\), then $$a^{\phi(n)}\equiv1\mod n$$
